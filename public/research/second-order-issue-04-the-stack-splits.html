The Stack Splits

In a single June fortnight Washington restricted its most capable models and Beijing released its best as open weights. The constraint that now governs the global AI standard has moved from capability, where the United States still leads, to diffusion, where US policy is working against it.

Read the companion deck (PDF)→

THE CLAIM

Since the series began we have read the AI buildout from inside the United States, where the capital and the frontier models sit. June forces a different question, not who builds the best model but who controls the one the world actually runs. The export controls intended to slow China are, on this month's evidence, slowing the United States instead. Washington suspended Anthropic's two most capable public models and held OpenAI's next release for review in the same fortnight that Zhipu's z.ai gave away a top-tier model as open weights trained on Chinese silicon. The capability gap has rarely been narrower, and the deployment gap just widened in China's favor.

Washington pulled the leaders. A June 12 export-control directive suspended Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for all foreign nationals, which forced a full shutdown; on June 25 the White House had OpenAI hold its GPT-5.6 line for an up-to-30-day pre-release review (Exhibit 1).

The model that was pulled is the best one. Fable 5 leads the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index at 60; the best model deployable without a carve-out scores 56, and China's best open model scores 51 (Exhibit 2).

The base layer has moved. Chinese open families draw roughly 229 million Hugging Face downloads a month against about 105 million for US open families, and Qwen alone, anchoring more than 113,000 derivative models, outweighs Google and Meta combined (Exhibit 3).

It runs without Nvidia. The GLM-5 family was trained on about 100,000 Huawei Ascend accelerators and no Nvidia GPUs, at a reported throughput cost of roughly a third (Exhibit 4). Open weights on domestic silicon is a complete stack beyond the reach of US export law.

For operators, the practical implication is to treat model access as a supply line rather than a subscription, because a model that can be suspended by directive is a single point of failure.

01 · WHAT HAPPENED

Washington restricts as Beijing opens

Two weeks in June reset the competitive map, and capability had little to do with it. On June 12 Anthropic received an export-control directive, issued on national-security grounds, to suspend all access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models for any foreign national, inside or outside the country and including its own foreign-national staff. Because no provider can separate foreign from domestic users in real time across a base of hundreds of millions, the order's practical effect was a complete shutdown, and Anthropic disabled both models for everyone. The stated concern was a reported method of jailbreaking Fable 5; the company's answer was that a narrow vulnerability should not justify recalling a model already deployed to hundreds of millions, and that the same test applied across the industry would halt frontier deployment altogether. Whatever the merits of the particular case, the precedent it sets matters more than the case: the most capable US models are now deployable only at the government's discretion.

Thirteen days later that discretion became a process. On June 25 the White House asked OpenAI to hold its GPT-5.6 line, the models known as Sol, Terra, and Luna, under an executive order that invites frontier laboratories to submit advanced models for government review up to thirty days before release. OpenAI complied, said it expected to ship within weeks, and objected that such a process should not become the default because it withholds the best tools from developers, enterprises, and allied governments. The objection is reasonable and the immediate outcome is unchanged: for now, US frontier deployment runs through a licensing queue (Exhibit 1).

Beijing's laboratories spent the same fortnight doing the opposite. On June 13 Zhipu, which sells internationally as z.ai, released GLM-5.2 as open weights under a permissive MIT license, free to download, run, and fine-tune; its launch note made the contrast its argument, observing that when access to frontier models is cut off for non-technical reasons the case for open models only strengthens. On June 18 Elon Musk put a date on the convergence, replying that Chinese models would reach the level of Anthropic's Fable by the first quarter of 2027, the same horizon z.ai sets for itself, so the figure reads as agreement rather than a Chinese boast. The people building these systems on both sides now expect an open Chinese model at the US frontier within roughly two quarters.

02 · THE REFRAME

The binding constraint moved from capability to policy

The US lead is real, narrow, and shrinking, and it is worth stating precisely. On the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index, Anthropic's Fable 5 leads at 60, Google's Gemini 3.1 Pro follows at 57, Anthropic's Opus 4.8 at 56, and OpenAI's GPT-5.5 at 55; GLM-5.2, open and free, scores 51 (Exhibit 2). The best model in the world is American, and the distance from the best deployable US model to the best open model is about five points. What changed in June did not widen that lead; it widened the gap in the other direction, because the top of the table is now the part American users cannot reach.

The opposing case deserves its strongest form. The frontier still belongs to US closed laboratories; benchmark scores are not revenue, and enterprise spending still flows overwhelmingly through closed APIs from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google; GLM-5.2's published numbers are in part vendor-reported and not fully verified; and June's restrictions may prove narrow and temporary, a jailbreak fix and a review queue rather than a wall. We accept all of it. Our claim is narrower, and we think harder to dismiss: the variable that decides who sets the standard has shifted from capability, where the United States leads, to deployment and diffusion, where US policy now subtracts and China adds. Developers build on whichever model they can deploy and hold, and that model becomes the standard, regardless of which one tops the table in a given week.

03 · THE BASE LAYER

Open weights compound through adoption, and adoption has flipped

The right scoreboard for an open-weight contest is not the leaderboard but the installed base, because open models compound through use. Every fine-tune, quantization, and integration built on a model raises the cost of moving off it, and that accumulation has tipped toward China. Hugging Face's own Spring 2026 review found Chinese models to be the plurality of all downloads at 41%, recorded Qwen overtaking Llama as the most-downloaded family, and noted that the industry's share of new model development fell from roughly 70% before 2022 to about 37% as independent developers, the people who fine-tune and redistribute open weights, rose to 39%. The base layer of the ecosystem is open, and it is increasingly Chinese.

Our own measurement agrees. Pulling the Hugging Face API on June 29, the four leading Chinese open families, Qwen, DeepSeek, GLM, and MiniMax, drew about 229 million downloads over the trailing month against roughly 105 million for the four leading US open families, Gemma, Llama, GPT-OSS, and Phi; Qwen on its own, at about 180 million, drew more than every US open family combined (Exhibit 3, Second Order calculations; download counts are inflated by automated pulls and measure scale rather than precise usage). The compounding shows in what gets built next: Qwen anchors more than 113,000 derivative models, and over 200,000 once every model that tags it is counted, which Hugging Face notes exceeds Google's and Meta's base families combined. Demand on the paid side points the same way; on OpenRouter, the largest neutral router, open-weight models reached roughly a third of all tokens by late 2025, with Chinese models the fastest-growing share.

Downloads are noisy, and we treat them as scale rather than truth, but three independent measures, downloads, derivative counts, and routed token volume, point the same way, and the mechanism is what matters. A developer who builds on Qwen writes prompts, tools, evaluations, and fine-tunes against Qwen, and the next project begins from that base. This is a network effect operating one layer below the model, in the tooling and habits that gather around whichever weights are free to hold, and it is accruing fastest to the Chinese families.

04 · THE FULL STACK

China routes around Nvidia too

Open weights would matter less if the models still depended on American hardware to exist, and increasingly they do not. Zhipu reports that the GLM-5 family, GLM-5.2 included, was trained on roughly 100,000 Huawei Ascend 910B accelerators on Huawei's MindSpore framework, with no Nvidia hardware at any stage (Exhibit 4). The claim is vendor-originated and we flag it as such, but it is consistent across several outlets and with the wider buildout of Huawei's CloudMatrix systems. The cost is real and measurable: reported inference runs at about 17 to 19 tokens per second on the Huawei stack against roughly 25 to 30 for Nvidia-backed peers, a penalty near a third, which we show as a range rather than a benchmark because the figures are reported and single-sourced.

Assembled, the three layers describe a stack that answers to no US authority: open weights anyone can hold, trained on domestic silicon, distributed through a platform on which Chinese models already lead. Hugging Face frames this as the emerging logic of AI sovereignty, governments and firms fine-tuning open models on their own data and running them on hardware they control. For a buyer in Riyadh, Jakarta, or Sao Paulo choosing between a US closed model that Washington can switch off and a Chinese open model that no one can, a third less throughput is a price many will pay for a supply line that cannot be cut.

SECOND-ORDER EFFECT: THE STANDARD IS SET BY WHAT PEOPLE BUILD ON

The first-order reading of June is a US victory: keep the best models at home and deny them to rivals. The second-order reading runs the other way, and it follows from how standards are actually won. They are won at the base layer, where availability matters more than peak capability, and they compound, because for every week a model is the one developers can hold and fine-tune, the tooling, the habits, and the next layer of work settle onto it.

So the restrictions work against their own intent. When the highest-scoring model can be withdrawn by directive, the rational builder hedges toward weights that cannot be recalled, which today means the Chinese open families. Washington is defending a capability lead by conceding the standard that the lead was meant to secure.

WHAT WE'D DO · THE OPERATOR'S PLAYBOOK

1. Treat model access as a supply line. Do not single-source a closed model that can be suspended by directive. For each critical AI workflow, name a fallback model and the trigger that moves you to it.

2. Stand up and test an open-weight fallback now. Even where a US closed model runs in production, keep a self-hostable open model in parallel, so the switching cost is a measured quantity rather than a discovery made under pressure.

3. Separate the weights from the jurisdiction. Open weights are borderless; a hosted API is not. Where a Chinese model fits, run the weights on infrastructure you control rather than its China-hosted endpoint, and keep regulated data off any jurisdiction you would not choose.

4. Build model-agnostic. Standardize internal tooling on interfaces that let the underlying model be swapped within a day, because both the leaderboard and the policy regime will keep moving.

5. Map the silicon beneath the stack. Know your exposure to Nvidia against the alternatives, and to the memory bottleneck behind all of it, the subject of next week's issue; concentration upstream is your risk as much as your supplier's.

6. If you sell outside the US, assume open. Non-US customers can and increasingly will build on Chinese open weights. Price, integrate, and position for a default model that is not American.

WATCHLIST · WHAT WOULD CHANGE OUR MIND

GPT-5.6 release. A broad release within weeks marks the review as a speed bump; continued delay marks it as a standing license regime.

Fable 5 and Mythos 5. Whether, and on what terms, they return. A quiet restoration argues a jailbreak fix; continued suspension argues policy.

Q1 2027. The horizon Musk and z.ai share for an open model at Fable's level. Watch the Artificial Analysis and LMArena gaps either close or stall.

Open-weight share. Hugging Face downloads and derivatives, and whether the US open efforts, GPT-OSS, Gemma, and OLMo, recover momentum.

Falsifier. A fast US carve-out that ships frontier models broadly, together with a plateau in Chinese open-weight adoption, would break the claim that the standard is moving east. We are watching both.

SCORECARD · CONSENSUS VS. THE TAPE

"The US is years ahead in AI." True of the single best model, by a few points. It is newly false of what can be deployed and of what the world builds on, which is where standards are set.

"Open source is a sideshow to the closed frontier." This is the opposite of true. Open weights are about a third of OpenRouter tokens and the plurality of Hugging Face downloads.

"Export controls slow China down." On chips the question is contested and beyond this issue; on models, June suggests the controls handed China its most effective open-source argument and pushed global builders toward weights that cannot be revoked. We will mark this against the tape as it develops.
