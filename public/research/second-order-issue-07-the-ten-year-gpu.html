The Ten-Year GPU

The next generation of hardware, networking, and serving software may extend the life of the last. In the public disclosures we could match, debt does not underwrite years six through ten.

Read the companion deck (PDF)→

Scope & method

This issue tests a claim in AI credit, argued most fully by investor Gavin Baker, that disaggregated inference, splitting a request across specialized chips, can extend a deployed GPU's economic life toward ten to fifteen years. The technical evidence is vendor demonstrations, announced architectures, and one research prototype; the financial evidence is the five operators that disclose enough to compare. We observe the architecture and the financing clocks. We do not observe an old fleet earning after year five inside such a system. That step remains the test.

THE CLAIM

The ten-year GPU is an old chip joined to a new system.

Prefill and decode make different demands on hardware, and splitting them across specialized pools joined by a fast fabric can keep an older card working after a monolithic cluster would have retired it. The pieces are being built: one operating same-generation case doubled H100 throughput, with the split itself worth about a 1.5 times step, and systems pairing different chips for each phase are announced but not yet fielded (Exhibit 1).

None of the operators we reviewed discloses an old fleet running inside such a system. Across five public operators, equipment lives cluster at five to six years, customer commitments at three to five, and GPU financing with disclosed maturities at two to six. Only IREN supplies a clean project-level match, and its debt ends no later than the Microsoft service fees (Exhibit 3).

Longer life therefore turns on another capital decision, and its payoff shows up in the financing. The old GPU needs a complementary accelerator, network, serving software, and a site where its power still earns. If that investment clears, the first effect is a higher advance rate, lenders funding more of each GPU against the same collateral, and a new residual market for years six through ten. If it fails, the product cycle stays a replacement cycle.

01 · THE ARCHITECTURE

New hardware may extend the life of old hardware

GPU private credit has been underwritten on three-to-four-year lives. On a May podcast, Gavin Baker argued that disaggregated inference could stretch that toward ten to fifteen years, moving Hopper or Ampere into prefill while a Cerebras system or Groq LPU handles the more latency-sensitive decode, and lowering financing costs toward five or six percent. He was naming a direction several vendors are already building and attaching a credit conclusion to it, which makes the steps between the architecture and the conclusion worth separating.

Prefill processes the prompt in parallel and is generally compute-bound. Decode generates one token at a time and leans more heavily on memory bandwidth and latency. A disaggregated system gives each phase its own workers and moves the KV cache between them, so the old card no longer has to meet the service requirement for the whole response. Which phase it should take is the open question. Prefill rewards cheap parallel compute and newer number formats, which points toward inexpensive current-generation silicon; decode rewards memory capacity and bandwidth, which points toward a high-memory older card. Baker's version sends Hopper or Ampere to prefill, but the memory-bound profile of decode makes the older card at least as plausible a decoder, and the one cross-vendor prototype we found assigns it there. The role is a hypothesis on either reading, and no operator has shown which one holds.

NVIDIA and Sarvam AI demonstrate the first step on H100 (Exhibit 1). At 75 tokens per second per user on one 30-billion-parameter model, the vendor-reported throughput index rose from 1.00 to 1.26 after kernel optimization, 1.31 after scheduling changes, and 2.00 after optimized 1P+1D disaggregation. Both pools used H100. NVIDIA attributes about a 1.5 times step to the disaggregation itself and ties the result to that model, workload shape, and latency target. AWS's deployment guidance draws the same boundary from the buyer's side: the split pays for long-context, high-concurrency serving, while short prompts and low concurrency stay on a single pool. In this vendor test, phase separation and orchestration raised throughput on H100 hardware for one model and one latency target.

AWS and Cerebras announced a Bedrock system that assigns prefill to AWS Trainium, decode to Cerebras CS-3, and the transfer between them to Amazon's Elastic Fabric Adapter. NVIDIA's Rubin and Groq 3 LPX architecture combines GPUs, LPUs, fabric logic, BlueField-4, and liquid cooling as one inference system. The authors report higher goodput and goodput per dollar across four Qwen3 models and three traces. Operator economics remain outside that research result.

We searched the same disclosures for the step that matters to credit, a post-year-five Hopper or Ampere fleet placed inside such a heterogeneous system beside newer specialized hardware, on prefill or decode, and found none, nor any report of the capital required to do it. What the public record holds so far is one operating same-generation vendor case, announced current-generation heterogeneous designs, and a cross-vendor research prototype, each joining specialized compute pools with a fabric and a scheduler, and each stopping short of the vintage-extension claim.

02 · POWER AND THE SITE

The network and the site become part of the GPU’s useful life

NVIDIA's Dynamo materials give the network as much space as the split itself, listing accelerated asynchronous transfer, request routing, dynamic GPU scheduling, and KV-cache offload alongside prefill-decode separation. AWS and Cerebras name EFA networking as the connection between their two systems, and HMA-Serve devotes much of its design to RDMA, data-format conversion, and hiding transfer behind computation. The emphasis follows the economics. Once a request splits across two pools, the old card's useful life is no longer a property of the card; it depends on the network that moves data between the pools and the site that powers them. A card fast enough for its phase still loses money if its cache moves too slowly, its data needs expensive conversion, or the serving layer cannot keep both pools busy, and a faster fabric can admit an old card into the heterogeneous pool or shut it out if the old server, software stack, or site cannot join at a reasonable cost.

Divide MLCommons throughput by NVIDIA's nameplate maximum system power and a Blackwell system carries about 2.26 times the completed tokens per kilowatt of a Hopper system, roughly 24,800 against 11,000 million tokens per megawatt-hour; nameplate power is a rated ceiling rather than measured draw, so the ratio is a proxy (Exhibit 2). A retained Hopper rack covers its modeled power and operating cost at under two cents per million tokens, across the plausible range of inference prices. In a power-bound site the decision is not whether the old rack earns, but whether the megawatt earns more on new hardware. In the model, below about thirteen cents per million completed tokens the old rack wins; above it, the extra Blackwell tokens repay the conversion and replacement wins while the old rack is still making money. Thirteen cents sits at the floor of realized token prices, below the twenty-eight cents per million that the cheapest open model charges for output and well under the roughly $1.75 market token index, though those anchors price different models and service tiers than the benchmarked workload, so the comparison is directional. If the extra Blackwell tokens find buyers anywhere near prevailing prices, a power-bound site leans toward replacement, and the old rack keeps earning mainly where power stays cheap. That modeled crossover sits between roughly ten and twenty-six cents as utilization and the payback period vary, and it moves lower still once the complementary accelerator, network, and integration capital that no operator discloses are counted.

Where power binds, the same megawatt earns about 2.26 times as much on new silicon, so the price of power and the cost of the site drive the decision, with the age of the chip a lesser factor. Where the site cannot take the denser system, an energized old rack may remain more useful than an empty position waiting for power. We still lack the operator behavior needed to sort those cases by facility.

The economic test has four terms: revenue from the workload assigned to the old GPU, its power and hosting cost, the capital required for the complementary system, and the value of the site power a replacement would use. The life-extension case requires the first term to cover the other three for several years. Physical survival by itself does not clear that test.

03 · THE DEBT CLOCKS

The disclosed GPU debt clocks in this sample end by year six

A GPU loan runs on three clocks: how long the equipment earns, how long the customer is committed to pay, and how long the debt runs. Matching them does not make a loan safe. It narrows one exposure, the merchant tail, by keeping repayment inside contracted cash flow; when the asset or the debt runs past the customer, the later years depend on a residual no one has yet priced. Deployment, termination, cost, concentration, amortization, and remarketing risks sit outside the match, which is why IREN's facility repays on a schedule inside the contract, ends no later than the final Microsoft payment, and takes security over the project GPUs, the project equity, and the contract cash flows.

Five public operators disclose enough to compare an equipment-life estimate with a customer commitment, and one discloses a clean project debt match (Exhibit 3). Equipment lives cluster at five to six years. Named or weighted customer commitments run three to five. GPU financing with disclosed maturities in the broader source set runs from two to six years. Three of the five asset-contract rows match exactly.

CoreWeave carries technology equipment for six years against a weighted-average committed-contract duration of about five at the end of 2025, an issuer-level comparison since neither measure maps a customer to a particular GPU pool, and its recent DDTL agreements use a six-year straight-line GPU Depreciated Amount, a contractual convention with no disclosed market-value or recovery claim. Nebius raised server life from four years to five in the first quarter of 2026, citing utilization commitments, and its named Microsoft and Meta agreements run five years. WhiteFiber carries cloud equipment for five years against a five-year Paris deployment, with a separate Iceland GPU loan that starts at two years and can extend to four. Applied Digital Cloud stretched most equipment finance leases to five-year amortization while its named second AI-customer agreement ran 36 months, a fleet-level proxy since no filing maps a lease to that customer.

IREN's HPC hardware carries a five-year life, its Microsoft GB300 service agreement averages five years, and approximately $3.6 billion of project financing matures on December 31, 2031 or earlier, whenever the last Microsoft service fee is paid. It is the only clean match in the sample, with asset, customer, and debt on one clock. The interim amortization schedule is private, though the outside maturity cannot cross the disclosed customer-payment boundary.

Of the GPU-linked debt disclosures with a stated maturity in this source set, none reaches year ten. Only IREN cleanly matches the asset, customer term, and project debt, and that debt ends no later than the Microsoft service fees. The other observations are issuer-level, fleet-level, or different-fleet proxies. Several large private GPU facilities omit maturity altogether. The available schedules therefore place no disclosed senior financing behind a later-life configuration.

04 · THE ACCOUNTING LIFE

Management has moved toward five years

When CoreWeave moved computing equipment from five years to six effective in 2023, it reduced that year's total expenses by $20 million; when Nebius moved servers from four years to five at the start of 2026, it cut quarterly depreciation by $43.1 million and lifted net income by $41.6 million. A depreciation life is an accounting estimate of the period over which an asset's benefits are consumed, neither a residual-value forecast nor an underwriting case, and, as those two moves show, it is endogenous: lengthening the life lowers reported depreciation and raises earnings. These are management's disclosed consumption assumptions, and they arrive with management's incentives. Every operator that revised the estimate in the past two years cited use and commitments, moved the life toward five, and stopped at six or under (Exhibit 3).

In May, CoreWeave said Blackwell, Hopper, and Ampere were all being monetized, with quarter-over-quarter price increases for A100, H100, H200, and L40 capacity and near-term supply largely sold out. IREN operated H100 and H200 fleets while procuring B200, B300, and GB300 systems, then tied its new GB300 deployment to Microsoft and a fresh project capital stack. Operators are monetizing or procuring several generations at once, though the filings do not place them in one system or assign them to separate inference phases.

WhiteFiber sold H200 servers carried at $24.3 million for $26.1 million in the first quarter of 2026, a $1.8 million gain, though the filing omits the age, count, buyer, and reason for sale: one transaction above book during a shortage, with the later-life curve unknown. Applied Digital Cloud reported all 6,144 deployed GPUs earning revenue at the end of 2025, while the unaudited forecast prepared for its sale assumed roughly 40 percent post-term price degradation per GPU-hour for next-generation equipment. One operator is extending the capital clock while underwriting a later price step-down, and neither disclosure shows whether complementary infrastructure keeps those GPUs productive after the first customer term.

In these five disclosure-rich operators, accounting lives and named customer terms cluster near five years. The visible GPU-linked debt clocks end by year six, though only IREN is cleanly matched. The proposed ten-year system has yet to appear in the disclosures we reviewed. That gap matters because the mechanism requires another deployment decision around the point where today's accounting life and customer contract end.

05 · CUMULATIVE COMPUTE

Complementary infrastructure can make compute cumulative

In the configuration Baker proposes, an old generation keeps working in a narrower role, on prefill or, as the one cross-vendor prototype does, on memory-bound decode, while a specialized accelerator handles the rest and the network carries the KV cache between them. That departs from the usual replacement cycle, in which each new generation clears the last as it arrives. Repeat it across several generations and the installed base grows through gross additions and retires fewer cards, so useful compute becomes cumulative.

From July 2024 to June 2026 the A100 public on-demand median fell 41.7 percent, from $3.07 to $1.79 per GPU-hour, while the H100 held roughly flat and the newer H200 and B200 rose from later starts (AIMultiple, 63 providers). Each generation is already trading on its own path while remaining available. The series exclude negotiated contracts and measure neither realized revenue nor resale value, so they support a direction and no more.

CoreWeave's sold-out account and WhiteFiber's H200 sale both fit a market where scarcity supports every generation at once. The cumulative-supply effect appears when deployable capacity catches up. A GPU that would have retired after five years instead moves to a narrower, lower-priced role, while the complementary accelerator adds its own output. Preserved supply does not stay in place: the likelier path is a cascade, new GPUs holding the power-constrained premium sites while older high-memory fleets move to cheaper power and lower inference tiers, with legacy low-density halls as their second homes. The reference class is used aircraft, servers, and mining rigs, where the price of the old asset falls until it just covers power, hosting, maintenance, and the opportunity cost of the site. If inference demand absorbs the extra supply at the old price, the residual owner keeps the added earning years; if it does not, customers receive cheaper compute.

The AWS-Cerebras and Rubin-Groq designs direct new spending toward fabric, specialized accelerators, and the software that coordinates them. If the architecture works economically, those suppliers would sell the enabling capital. Operators could gain more output from the installed base while taking on integration risk, and customers could gain another service tier. Senior lenders receive protection only where the new system, customer renewal, and refinancing enter their documents.

Applied Digital Cloud's 40 percent post-term assumption is one forecast of the price step-down. The A100's 41.7 percent public-rate decline is one observed vintage path during a period of changing provider mix and scarcity. Neither measures a life extension or establishes causation. They show only that an older vintage can remain listed while its public asking rate falls, and that one operator underwrites a later price step-down.

06 · THE NEXT TEST

The next test is an old GPU inside a new system

IREN runs H100 and H200 beside its new GB300 program, and CoreWeave monetizes Ampere and Hopper on a six-year clock, which makes them the places to watch for the first real evidence: an operator disclosing that it has moved an older generation into such a heterogeneous system, on prefill or decode, beside newer specialized hardware, with the throughput, power, and revenue to show what the change earned against what it cost. Confirming the economics would take several cohorts, workloads, sites, and quarters of realized results, but the first disclosure could arrive in a quarterly filing well before any GPU turns ten.

None of the filings we reviewed carries that record yet. A real test needs the old fleet's vintage and deployment date, the complementary hardware and network and site work and the capital to connect it, the throughput and utilization and power and realized revenue before and after redeployment, and the contract or refinancing that carries the later period. Until an operator puts those together, the extension stays a design without a cash flow behind it.

A customer contract renewed beyond year five would extend contracted revenue into the later period, and a refinancing of the same aging fleet, supported by that renewal and a disclosed heterogeneous architecture, would be the moment senior lenders put capital behind the life-extension case. Both may surface in the financing record before any operating disclosure describes the architecture, though an operator funding the retrofit from cash would leave that record silent. The absence of ten-year debt bears on current bankability, while economic life is a separate matter: if the loan repays in five years and the fleet earns for three more, those later cash flows accrue to equity as an unlevered option, and they sit outside the credit base case until an operator demonstrates them. A facility that ends with its first customer leaves the later experiment, and its residual risk, with the owner.

If operators deploy heterogeneous systems and still retire Hopper and Ampere on the old schedule, the physical case does not hold. The economic case fails earlier, when the complementary accelerator, fabric, conversion, and the power a denser system would have used consume more value than the old fleet preserves. Either outcome leaves the residual value now carried in GPU-backed debt close to where the five-year clocks already place it.

SUMMARY · THE READ

The ten-year GPU is a second capital project

One vendor case doubled H100 output with kernel work, scheduling, and a prefill-decode split together; the split alone was worth about 1.5 times. AWS-Cerebras and NVIDIA Rubin-Groq are announced current-generation heterogeneous designs, and one preprint reports a cross-vendor transfer implementation. None observes post-year-five Hopper or Ampere economics.

The financing record ends earlier. Five operators cluster around five-year equipment and customer clocks, disclosed GPU maturities end between years two and six, and only IREN cleanly ties the outside debt date to the initial customer payments. The later life, if it arrives, will require a new network, complementary compute, integration work, and another financing or ownership decision. The old GPU may well keep working. Its financial life will depend on the return on the new system built around it.

THE SECOND-ORDER EFFECT · LONGER LIFE BUILDS A RESIDUAL MARKET BEFORE IT CUTS SPREADS

CoreWeave priced two GPU facilities seven weeks apart at very different spreads. The investment-grade DDTL 4.0 borrowed at SOFR plus 225; the sub-investment-grade DDTL 5.0, backed by two weaker customers and a parent guarantee, borrowed at SOFR plus 450. That 225-basis-point gap moves with customer quality, recourse, distribution, and covenant structure; the public documents give no way to isolate a residual-value component, and neither facility discloses a lender advance against years six through ten (Exhibit 4). So if the technical case ever clears and a ten-year residual is proven, the first effect would be a higher advance rate and a slower amortization schedule, well before it cut the coupon.

IREN and the CoreWeave facilities are already the senior half of such a structure: output-backed project finance, a claim on committed customer payments that amortizes inside the contract and sits with banks, project lenders, and eventually asset-backed investors. The missing half is a different kind of lending. Residual asset finance advances against what the machine itself is worth after its first contract, funds the complementary hardware, and is repaid by renewal, redeployment, or resale in years six through ten; underwriting it means holding the whole productive system as collateral, with a reserve for the midlife retrofit and the right to move or re-equip the fleet. If that market forms, it may grow the way aircraft finance grew, from contract-backed equipment trusts into specialist lessors, certified appraisers, maintenance reserves, and pooled securitizations, similar structures built for the same problem, a long residual on expensive equipment (Exhibit 5). We raise the parallel because aircraft is the one large market where lenders learned to price that residual; ISTAT's appraiser program materials and the standard primers on equipment trust certificates are good places to start. Most of the gain would pass through: cheaper capital expands capacity, competition hands the efficiency to customers as cheaper compute, and older fleets slide toward lower tiers and cheaper power, the cascade in Section 05.

The same names recur across these deals, Apollo and Blackstone as capital, NVIDIA as vendor, and a short list of labs as customers (Exhibit 6), and that concentration is the risk: a residual market built on one architecture and one inference-price curve would reprice everywhere at once. No year-six cash flow exists yet, so dispersion in lenders' residual marks is what independent judgment looks like, and convergence would mean underwriting had been replaced by one shared model. We would see the market arrive in the first facility that lends against year six on a disclosed residual.
