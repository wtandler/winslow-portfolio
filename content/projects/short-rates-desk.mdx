## The Problem

A rates PM is paid for having a view before the desk does, and defending it when they push back. The apparent obstacle is volume: sell-side research from a dozen banks, economist Substacks, Fed releases, NBER and SSRN papers, prediction-market odds, and the prints themselves. The obvious fix is more reading, or a bigger research team to do the reading. Large funds buy exactly that, and it still costs them hours every morning.

I think that misreads the constraint. None of this information is hidden, so access isn't the problem. The bottleneck is that the signal is scattered across sources that each say a piece of it, and reading enough of them to be coherent is what takes the morning.

## The Insight

I settled on a simpler model of the market: there are only four voices in it, and the value is not in any one of them. It is in where they disagree.

* What **markets** say: Polymarket and Kalshi odds on Fed / CPI / NFP.
* What **dealers** say: the NY Fed's Survey of Market Expectations.
* What the **Street** is writing: bank desks, economists, independent researchers.
* What the **data** says: my own model.

Read alone, any single source is mostly noise. Read side by side, the gaps between them are the opportunities. So I didn't build a better reader. I built a divergence detector on public data, and that reframing is what lets one person replace a research desk's morning.

## What It Does

* **Today's View**: a proprietary forecast plus a one-paragraph thesis in a senior-strategist voice.
* **Consensus vs Our View**: Markets / Dealers / Street / Our Model, side by side, with divergence flags.
* **Event Feed**: a real-time chronological feed of Fed events, data prints, rate moves, and research drops, each synthesized with attribution.
* **Prediction Markets**: Kalshi and Polymarket probability bars on the relevant contracts.
* **Street Synthesis**: firm-by-firm positioning (Barclays / Goldman / JPM) with consensus-vs-divergence flags.
* **Morning and Close Notes**: twice-daily synthesis, 7:00 AM ET pre-open and 4:15 PM ET post-close, emailed to subscribers.

## What It Hinges On

The product lives or dies on one thing: whether a PM trusts the synthesis enough to act on it. A PM will not trade a number they cannot trace, so a synthesized view nobody can source back is worth nothing on a desk. That single constraint drove the two hard decisions below. The interesting problem was never the UI.

## The Hard Part

First, ingestion. To pull structured signal out of DTCC, OFR, FINRA TRACE, NY Fed percentiles, prediction-market APIs, and free-form sell-side commentary, I wrote a custom adapter per source, plus a clustering step to dedupe the same idea showing up in ten places at once.

Second, defensibility, which is where I paid for the trust constraint. Every synthesized claim carries attribution back to its source. I run the model work in tiers with a cross-model check rather than one large call: Claude Haiku for fast, event-driven Tier 1 synthesis; Claude Opus for the scheduled morning and close notes and for position extraction; and a GPT pass as cross-model confirmation on Tier 1 output before anything publishes. Each model does the job it is cheapest at doing well, and nothing reaches a subscriber on a single model's word.

## The Architecture

A Python worker does the heavy lifting (ingest, clustering, synthesis, the LLM notes) and writes to Postgres on Neon. The Next.js frontend on Vercel reads Postgres only, so I kept the read path fast and dumb. I run scheduling on GitHub Actions cron, which removes a whole category of always-on infrastructure to babysit. Email goes through Resend. An admin intake console behind HTTP Basic Auth lets me paste, upload, and override any note that failed.

## Building with AI

This is a single-builder attempt at something that normally takes a team, and the honest accounting is that the leverage is real but bounded. AI-assisted ingestion and summarization let me carry the reading load of a research desk. What they do not supply is the judgment, so the work went into guardrails: public data only, attribution on every claim, and a cross-model confirmation pass. The point of those is not polish. It is that the morning note has to be something a PM can act on without re-checking it, or the product has no reason to exist.

## What I Learned

* **The value is in the disagreement.** Any single source on its own is mostly noise; the product is the comparison, not the reading.
* **Attribution is non-negotiable in finance.** A synthesized view nobody can trace is worthless on a desk, and that constraint, not the interface, shaped the build.
* **Tiered and cross-model beats one big call.** Cheap fast models for the feed, expensive careful models for the notes, a second model to confirm.
* **GitHub Actions is an underrated scheduler.** For a cron-driven data product it removed an entire layer of infrastructure.
